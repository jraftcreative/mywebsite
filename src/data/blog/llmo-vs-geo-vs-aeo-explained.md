If you've spent any time researching how to get your business recommended by ChatGPT or Gemini, you've probably run into a wall of acronyms: GEO, LLMO, AEO, AIO. Each blog you read seems to use a different one, and none of them bother explaining how they relate to each other.

Here's the short version: they all describe the same discipline. The strategies are identical. The difference is purely in framing.

But the long version is worth understanding, because the term you choose affects how you communicate with agencies, how you evaluate proposals, and how you search for information yourself.

## What Each Term Actually Means

Let's define all four, starting with the broadest and narrowing down.

### GEO — Generative Engine Optimisation

GEO is the most widely adopted term as of 2026. It describes the practice of optimising your brand's digital presence so that **generative AI platforms** — ChatGPT, Google Gemini, Perplexity, Microsoft Copilot, and Google AI Overviews — recommend your business when users ask for suggestions.

The word "generative" is key. It covers any platform that generates answers rather than listing links. This makes GEO the broadest term: it applies to every AI search tool, regardless of the underlying technology.

GEO is the term most agencies have settled on, including us. If you want to see what GEO looks like in practice, we cover it in detail on our [Generative Engine Optimisation](/generative-engine-optimisation-singapore/) page.

### LLMO — Large Language Model Optimisation

LLMO frames the same discipline from a technology perspective. Instead of focusing on what the AI platforms do (generate answers), it focuses on the technology powering them: **large language models**.

The practical work is identical to GEO. You're still optimising for the same platforms, using the same strategies. But the framing appeals to a more technical audience — CTOs, developers, and data-driven marketers who want to understand the mechanism, not just the outcome.

Some agencies prefer LLMO because it sounds more precise. Others avoid it because "large language model" means nothing to most business owners. We offer dedicated [LLMO services](/llmo-services-singapore/) for clients who prefer this framing.

### AEO — Answer Engine Optimisation

AEO positions AI search platforms as "answer engines" — tools that give direct answers rather than lists of results. The term is more common in US marketing circles and predates the current AI wave by a few years. Originally, AEO referred to optimising for Google's featured snippets and voice assistants like Alexa and Siri.

Since 2024, AEO has been repurposed to include ChatGPT, Gemini, and other AI platforms. The strategies overlap almost entirely with GEO and LLMO, but the framing is narrower: it emphasises the Q&A nature of AI interactions.

### AIO — AI Overview Optimisation

AIO is the most specific term. It refers exclusively to optimising for **Google's AI Overviews** — the AI-generated summaries that appear at the top of certain Google search results.

Google AI Overviews now trigger on roughly 48% of all Google searches, so this is a meaningful channel. But AIO ignores ChatGPT, Perplexity, Gemini (outside of Google Search), and Copilot. If you only optimise for AI Overviews, you're leaving a significant portion of AI search traffic on the table.

## Are the Strategies Actually Different?

No. And this is the most important thing to understand.

Regardless of whether an agency calls it GEO, LLMO, AEO, or AIO, the underlying work involves the same core activities:

**Entity optimisation** — making sure AI models understand exactly what your business is, what you offer, and where you're located. This means consistent NAP (name, address, phone) data, a well-structured About page, and mentions across authoritative third-party sources.

**Structured data** — implementing schema markup so AI platforms can extract information from your site programmatically. Organisation schema, LocalBusiness schema, FAQ schema, and service-specific schemas all play a role.

**Citation-ready content** — writing content that AI can quote directly. This means clear, factual statements rather than vague marketing language. When ChatGPT recommends a business, it often pulls a sentence or two verbatim from a webpage. Your content needs to be ready for that.

**Authority building** — earning mentions, reviews, and citations on platforms that AI models reference. For Singapore businesses, this includes Google Business Profile, industry directories, media coverage, and platform-specific review sites.

**Information freshness** — keeping your content current. AI models weight recency, so a pricing page last updated in 2023 will lose out to a competitor who updated theirs last month.

If someone tries to sell you "LLMO" as something fundamentally different from "GEO," ask them to explain the strategic difference. If they can't point to specific activities that differ, they're repackaging the same service under a different label.

## Why Does the Terminology Confusion Exist?

Three reasons, all of them understandable.

**The discipline is genuinely new.** AI search optimisation only emerged as a distinct practice in 2023-2024. When a new field appears, multiple communities independently coin their own terms. This happened with SEO in the late 1990s (it was also called "search engine placement," "search engine positioning," and "web positioning" before the industry settled on SEO).

**Different marketing communities have different preferences.** The SEO community tends toward GEO because it mirrors the structure of the term they already know. The AI/tech community prefers LLMO because it references the technology. The content marketing community leans toward AEO because it frames the discipline around answers and user intent.

**Agencies use whatever resonates with their client base.** An agency serving tech startups might lead with LLMO. An agency serving local businesses might use GEO. An agency that previously focused on featured snippets might rebrand their existing AEO services. None of this is dishonest — it's just marketing.

## Which Term Should You Use?

If you're a business owner looking for this service, **use GEO**. Here's why:

**Search volume supports it.** Google Trends data shows "generative engine optimisation" consistently outperforming "large language model optimisation" and "answer engine optimisation" in search interest, particularly in Asia-Pacific markets.

**It's the broadest term.** GEO covers all AI platforms — ChatGPT, Gemini, Perplexity, Copilot, and AI Overviews. You're not limiting yourself to one technology (LLMO), one interaction model (AEO), or one platform (AIO).

**Agencies have largely converged on it.** As of early 2026, the majority of agencies offering AI search optimisation services use GEO as their primary term. If you search for "GEO agency," you'll find more options — and more relevant results — than if you search for "LLMO agency."

That said, if you're evaluating agencies and one uses LLMO while another uses GEO, **don't use the terminology as a deciding factor**. Evaluate the actual strategies they propose, the case studies they can show, and their understanding of your specific industry.

## What Actually Matters (Regardless of the Name)

Strip away the acronyms and focus on these five signals. They're what AI models actually use to decide which businesses to recommend:

### 1. Entity Clarity

AI needs to understand your business as a distinct entity. This means your brand name, services, location, and unique selling points should be consistently stated across your website, Google Business Profile, social media, and third-party sites.

### 2. Structured Data

Schema markup gives AI platforms machine-readable information about your business. At minimum, implement Organisation, LocalBusiness, and FAQ schema. Industry-specific schemas (Restaurant, EducationalOrganisation, ProfessionalService) give you a further edge.

### 3. Content Extractability

Write content that AI can quote. Use clear headings, direct statements, and factual claims. Avoid burying key information inside paragraphs of filler. If your best selling point is "15 years serving Singapore SMEs," make that a standalone sentence, not a clause buried in a paragraph about your company history.

### 4. Third-Party Authority

AI models cross-reference your claims against third-party sources. Google reviews, industry directory listings, media mentions, and platform-specific reviews (Burpple for F&B, KiasuParents for education, Clutch for agencies) all contribute to your authority score.

### 5. Information Freshness

Outdated content gets deprioritised. Update your key pages at least quarterly. Publish new content monthly. Keep your Google Business Profile current with recent photos, updated hours, and new posts.

## The Singapore Context

In Singapore specifically, a few dynamics are worth noting.

**AI adoption is exceptionally high.** With 60.9% of the population already using generative AI, Singapore businesses face more urgency than those in most other markets. Your competitors are likely already thinking about this.

**"GEO" is the dominant search term locally.** While LLMO and AEO appear in Singapore search data, GEO consistently leads. If you're searching for agencies or information, you'll find more Singapore-relevant results by searching for GEO.

**The local agency market is catching up fast.** In 2024, very few Singapore agencies offered dedicated AI search optimisation. By early 2026, it's becoming a standard service offering. This means you have options — but it also means due diligence matters, as some agencies have more experience than others.
